5 Things Ruining Your Sleep That Have Nothing to Do with Screen Time

The first thing anyone says when you mention bad sleep: "Try putting your phone down an hour before bed." And yes, blue light can affect melatonin production. That is real.

But if you are still sleeping badly after doing that, the problem is probably not your screen. It is your bedroom. Specifically, these five things.

1. Noise You Do Not Remember Hearing

Your brain processes sound even while you sleep. A noise does not have to fully wake you up to disrupt your sleep cycle. Sounds as low as 40 dB (about the volume of a quiet conversation) can pull you from deep sleep into lighter sleep without you ever realizing it.

Common offenders: HVAC systems cycling on and off, a partner snoring, traffic outside, a dog barking down the street, plumbing sounds, garbage trucks. You may not remember any of these in the morning, but your sleep data tells the story. A noise event at 2 AM followed by 90 minutes of restless sleep is not a coincidence.

2. Room Temperature

The ideal sleep temperature for most adults is between 60-67°F (15-19°C). Most people sleep in rooms that are too warm. Your body needs to drop its core temperature by about 2-3 degrees to initiate and maintain sleep. If your room is 74°F, your body is fighting physics.

The tricky part is that room temperature changes throughout the night. Your heater cycles off at midnight. The sun starts warming the room at 5 AM. A partner's body heat raises the temperature under the covers. These shifts are invisible unless something is tracking them.

3. Humidity

This one surprises people. Humidity between 30-50% is ideal for sleep. Below 30%, your airways dry out, which can lead to snoring, dry mouth, and restless sleep. Above 50%, the air feels heavy, sweat does not evaporate efficiently, and your body has trouble cooling down.

If you live in a climate with seasons, your indoor humidity swings dramatically between winter (often below 20% with heating) and summer (often above 60% without dehumidification). Most people never measure it.

4. Inconsistent Noise, Not Just Loud Noise

Constant noise is less disruptive than inconsistent noise. A fan running all night at 45 dB is far less damaging to sleep than a room that alternates between silence and 45 dB. Your brain reacts to changes in sound level, not just the sound itself.

This is why white noise machines work. They do not make the room quieter. They make the room more consistent. They fill in the gaps so that when a car passes outside, the relative change in volume is smaller and your brain is less likely to respond.

5. Your Own Snoring

About 45% of adults snore at least occasionally. Most of them have no idea how often or how loudly. Snoring can fragment your own sleep even if it does not fully wake you. It is also one of the early indicators of sleep apnea, which is seriously underdiagnosed.

If you sleep alone, you have no way of knowing you snore unless something is listening. Most people find out from a partner. But if you do not have a partner in the room, you could be snoring for years without any awareness.
