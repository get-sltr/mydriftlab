Myth Busted Feb 25, 2026

Sleep Scores Are Useless Without Context

You check your wrist in the morning. Your sleep score says 72. Yesterday it was 78. The day before, 65.

What are you supposed to do with that?

Sleep scores have become the default language of sleep tracking. Every major wearable, every app, every smart ring gives you one. A single number between 0 and 100 that is supposed to tell you how well you slept. But this number has a fundamental problem: it tells you what happened without telling you why.

The Problem with a Number

A sleep score is a composite. Most apps calculate it from some combination of total sleep time, time spent in deep sleep, REM sleep, awakenings, heart rate, and movement. The exact formula varies by brand and is usually proprietary, which means your Fitbit 72 and your Oura 72 are measuring different things entirely.

But even setting aside the inconsistency, the bigger issue is that a score does not help you improve. Knowing you got a 65 tells you the night was below average. It does not tell you whether the problem was noise, temperature, late caffeine, stress, alcohol, a partner's snoring, or your dog jumping on the bed at 3 AM.

A score without a cause is just an anxiety number.

What Would Actually Be Useful

Compare these two morning experiences:

Typical Sleep Tracker

Sleep score: 65. You spent 1h 12m in deep sleep. You woke up 3 times. Total sleep: 6h 38m.

DriftLab Report

Noise event at 1:47 AM (72 dB, 6 min). Room temp rose to 76°F by 2:30 AM. Snoring detected 11:45 PM - 12:20 AM. Light sleep dominated after 2 AM.

The first one tells you the night was rough. The second one tells you why. The noise event was probably the neighbor's car alarm. The temperature spike means your AC cycled off. The snoring in the first hour might mean you ate too late or were congested.

Now you have something to work with. You can buy earplugs or a white noise machine. You can adjust the thermostat. You can try elevating your pillow. And you can measure whether those changes actually move the needle.

Scores Create the Wrong Motivation

There is a well-documented phenomenon called orthosomnia, where people become so fixated on optimizing their sleep score that the anxiety of tracking itself disrupts their sleep. They lie in bed stressing about whether they are falling asleep fast enough, whether their heart rate is low enough, whether they will hit their deep sleep target.

This is the natural result of giving people a grade without a rubric. When you do not know what drives the number, you try to control everything. When you know the specific cause, you can focus on that one thing and ignore the rest.

Context Changes Everything

A 65 after a night where your neighbor had a party is completely different from a 65 on a quiet Tuesday. A 78 in a 68°F room means something different from a 78 in a 76°F room. The score is the same, but the story is not.

This is why DriftLab does not lead with a score. It leads with a timeline. A full account of your night, showing what happened and when. Noise events timestamped. Temperature changes charted. Snoring episodes measured. If you want a summary number, it is there. But it is never the headline, because the headline should always be the thing you can actually change.

Better Data, Better Sleep

The goal is not to gamify sleep. The goal is to understand it. When you can see that your worst nights consistently have the same environmental pattern (too warm after midnight, noise events between 2 and 3 AM, snoring in the first hour), you stop guessing and start fixing.

That is the difference between tracking and understanding. One gives you a number. The other gives you an answer.
